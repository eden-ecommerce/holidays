# Christian Holidays: A Complete Cross-Denominational Catalogue

*A structured reference document for building an indexed, filterable directory of Christian holidays, feasts, fasts, seasons, and observances across all major traditions.*

---

## Introduction

This catalogue covers significant dates across the full breadth of Christianity — from the Roman Catholic solemnity calendar to the Twelve Great Feasts of Eastern Orthodoxy, the distinctive holidays of the Ethiopian and Armenian churches, and the observances of Protestant, Anglican, Adventist, and Evangelical traditions. It is designed as source data for a website tool that allows users to browse, search, and filter by multiple criteria.

**Scope:** Approximately 200 entries covering holidays, feasts, fasts, liturgical seasons, regional traditions, and commemoration days. Each entry includes structured metadata for filtering.

**Denominations covered:** Roman Catholic · Eastern Orthodox (Greek, Russian, Serbian, etc.) · Coptic Orthodox · Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo · Armenian Apostolic · Syriac Orthodox · Anglican / Church of England · Episcopal Church (USA) · Lutheran (ELCA & LCMS) · Methodist / Wesleyan · Presbyterian / Reformed · Baptist / Evangelical · Pentecostal / Charismatic · Seventh-day Adventist

## The Christian Liturgical Year: A Brief Overview

Most historic Christian traditions organize worship around a **liturgical year** that traces the life, death, and resurrection of Jesus Christ through recurring seasons and feasts. The year can be understood through three broad cycles:

**The Christmas Cycle** — anchored to the fixed date of December 25 (or January 6–7 in some Eastern traditions). It includes Advent, Christmas, and the Epiphany season.

**The Easter Cycle** — anchored to the moveable date of Easter Sunday. It includes Lent, Holy Week, Easter, Ascension, and Pentecost. The date of Easter is calculated differently in Western and Eastern Christianity (see note below).

**Ordinary Time** — the periods between the two cycles. Some traditions use this term; others call it "Trinitytide," "the Season after Pentecost," or number Sundays after Pentecost.

> **Easter Date Calculation:** Western churches (Catholic, Anglican, Lutheran, Methodist, Protestant) calculate Easter as the first Sunday after the first full moon on or after the spring equinox (March 21). Eastern Orthodox churches use the same formula but applied to the Julian calendar, which currently runs 13 days behind the Gregorian calendar, so Orthodox Easter (Pascha) typically falls 1–5 weeks later than Western Easter, though occasionally on the same date.

### Advent
*Also called: Adviento (Spanish), Avent (French), Der Advent (German)*

| Date | Type | Date Type | Denominations | Season | Region | Color | Themes |
|------|------|-----------|---------------|--------|--------|-------|--------|
| Four Sundays before Christmas (begins late Nov/early Dec) | Liturgical Season | Semi-fixed | Pan-Christian (liturgical traditions) | Advent | Global | Purple/Violet; Blue (Anglican, Methodist, some Lutheran) | Incarnation, Penitence, Eschatology |

A season of preparation, expectation, and watchfulness lasting four Sundays before Christmas. Advent marks the beginning of the liturgical year in Western Christianity. The tradition of lighting Advent candles — one for each Sunday — is widely observed across Catholic, Lutheran, Anglican, and Methodist churches. In its dual focus, Advent looks back to Christ's first coming at Christmas and forward to his promised Second Coming.

### Christmas Day / Nativity of Jesus Christ
*Also called: Nativity of Our Lord, Noel, Navidad, Natale, Рождество (Rozhdestvo), Genna/Ledet (Ethiopian), Dznoont (Armenian)*

| Date | Type | Date Type | Denominations | Season | Region | Color | Themes |
|------|------|-----------|---------------|--------|--------|-------|--------|
| December 25 (Western & New Calendar Orthodox); January 7 (Julian Calendar Orthodox, Coptic, Ethiopian); January 6 (Armenian) | Major Feast, Solemnity (Catholic) | Fixed | Pan-Christian | Christmas | Global | White, Gold | Incarnation, Birth of Christ |

The annual celebration of the birth of Jesus Christ in Bethlehem, as narrated in the Gospels of Matthew and Luke. The date of December 25 was established in Rome by the 4th century; Eastern Christians following the Julian calendar celebrate on January 7 in the Gregorian reckoning. The Armenian Apostolic Church uniquely celebrates Christmas and Epiphany together on January 6, preserving an ancient practice predating the 4th-century separation of the two feasts. Observed with special liturgies, carol services, nativity scenes, and family celebrations across all denominations.

### Christmas Season / Christmastide / The Twelve Days of Christmas

| Date | Type | Date Type | Denominations | Season | Region | Color | Themes |
|------|------|-----------|---------------|--------|--------|-------|--------|
| December 25 – January 5 (or through Epiphany, Jan 6) | Liturgical Season | Fixed | Pan-Christian (liturgical) | Christmas | Global | White, Gold | Incarnation, Joy |

The period from Christmas Day through the eve of Epiphany, known as the "Twelve Days of Christmas." In Catholic tradition, the Christmas season extends until the Feast of the Baptism of the Lord. Many traditions observe the days between Christmas and Epiphany as a continuous celebration, with special feasts on December 26 (St. Stephen), December 27 (St. John), and December 28 (Holy Innocents).

### Epiphany
*Also called: Twelfth Night, Three Kings Day, Día de Reyes (Spanish), La Befana (Italy), Theophany (Eastern), Timkat (Ethiopian)*

| Date | Type | Date Type | Denominations | Season | Region | Color | Themes |
|------|------|-----------|---------------|--------|--------|-------|--------|
| January 6 (fixed); or transferred to the nearest Sunday (some countries) | Solemnity (Catholic), Major Feast, Principal Holy Day (Anglican) | Fixed | Pan-Christian | Epiphany | Global | White, Gold | Life of Christ, Revelation, Marian (Theophany adds Baptism of Christ) |

The feast commemorating the manifestation of Christ to the world. In Western Christianity (Catholic, Protestant, Anglican), Epiphany primarily celebrates the arrival of the Magi at Bethlehem. In Eastern Christianity, it is called Theophany and commemorates Christ's Baptism in the Jordan River, with a blessing of waters as a central rite. In Hispanic and Latino cultures, January 6 is "Día de Reyes" (Three Kings Day), when children traditionally receive gifts. In Italy, the gift-giving figure is "La Befana." In some European Catholic countries, Epiphany is a more significant gift-giving day than Christmas.

### Ash Wednesday
*Also called: The Beginning of Lent, Dies Cinerum (Latin)*

| Date | Type | Date Type | Denominations | Season | Region | Color | Themes |
|------|------|-----------|---------------|--------|--------|-------|--------|
| 46 days before Easter (the Wednesday before the first Sunday of Lent) | Fast Day, Principal Holy Day | Moveable | Catholic, Anglican, Lutheran, Methodist, some Presbyterian and Baptist | Lent | Global (Western Christianity) | Purple/Violet | Penitence, Mortality, Covenant |

The first day of Lent in Western Christianity, marked by the imposition of ashes on the forehead in the shape of a cross, accompanied by the words "Remember that you are dust, and to dust you shall return" (Genesis 3:19). The ashes are traditionally made from palm branches burned from the previous year's Palm Sunday. Ash Wednesday is one of two required days of fasting and abstinence in Catholic practice (along with Good Friday). Eastern Orthodox churches begin Lent on Clean Monday, two days earlier, without the use of ashes.

### Lent
*Also called: Great Lent (Orthodox), Cuaresma (Spanish), Carême (French), Fastenzeit (German), Great Fast, Medz Bahk (Armenian), Hudade (Ethiopian)*

| Date | Type | Date Type | Denominations | Season | Region | Color | Themes |
|------|------|-----------|---------------|--------|--------|-------|--------|
| 40 days before Easter (from Ash Wednesday in the West; Clean Monday in the East) | Liturgical Season | Moveable | Pan-Christian (liturgical) | Lent | Global | Purple/Violet | Penitence, Baptism preparation, Passion of Christ |

A solemn season of fasting, prayer, and almsgiving in preparation for Easter. In Western Christianity, Lent spans 40 days from Ash Wednesday to Holy Saturday (Sundays are not counted as fasting days). In Eastern Orthodox tradition, Great Lent begins on Clean Monday and is stricter, involving abstinence from meat, dairy, fish, oil, and wine on most days, lasting 7 weeks. Ethiopian Orthodox Lent (Hudade) spans 55 days. The 40-day period recalls Christ's 40-day fast in the wilderness (Matthew 4:1–11).

### Palm Sunday / Passion Sunday
*Also called: Domingo de Ramos (Spanish), Flowery Sunday, Hosanna Sunday, Entry of Christ into Jerusalem*

| Date | Type | Date Type | Denominations | Season | Region | Color | Themes |
|------|------|-----------|---------------|--------|--------|-------|--------|
| Sunday before Easter | Major Feast | Moveable | Pan-Christian | Holy Week | Global | Red | Passion-Death, Life of Christ |

The beginning of Holy Week, commemorating Christ's triumphal entry into Jerusalem on a donkey, greeted by crowds waving palm branches (John 12:12–15). Congregations typically process with palm fronds (or pussy willow branches in Russia; olive branches in some Mediterranean countries) that are blessed during the liturgy. The liturgy also incorporates the reading of the Passion narrative, which gives this day its alternative name "Passion Sunday" in the Roman Rite. In the Philippine tradition, palaspás (ornately woven palms) are central to the observance.

---

### Holy Week
*Also called: Semana Santa (Spanish), Semaine Sainte (French), Karwoche (German), Mahal na Araw (Filipino), Mamat (Ethiopian)*

| Date | Type | Date Type | Denominations | Season | Region | Color | Themes |
|------|------|-----------|---------------|--------|--------|-------|--------|
| Palm Sunday through Holy Saturday | Liturgical Season | Moveable | Pan-Christian | Holy Week | Global | Red (Palm Sunday, Maundy Thursday), Purple (Lent days), Black (Good Friday) | Passion-Death, Life of Christ |

The final week of Lent, encompassing the most solemn liturgical days of the Christian year. It begins with Palm Sunday, moves through Maundy Thursday (Last Supper), Good Friday (Crucifixion), and Holy Saturday (the Great Silence before resurrection), culminating in the Easter Vigil. In Catholic Spain and Latin America, Holy Week (Semana Santa) is marked by elaborate public processions of images and hooded brotherhoods, making it one of the world's most dramatic religious observances. In the Philippines, Holy Week (Semana Santa) is the most important religious period of the year.

---

### Maundy Thursday / Holy Thursday
*Also called: Jueves Santo, Jeudi Saint, Gründonnerstag, Quinta-feira da Ceia, Holy and Great Thursday (Orthodox), Covenant Thursday*

| Date | Type | Date Type | Denominations | Season | Region | Color | Themes |
|------|------|-----------|---------------|--------|--------|-------|--------|
| Thursday of Holy Week | Feast | Moveable | Pan-Christian | Holy Week / Triduum | Global | White, then Red or Black stripped at end | Passion-Death, Incarnation, Covenant |

The commemoration of the Last Supper of Jesus with his disciples, at which he instituted the Eucharist ("the Lord's Supper"), washed the disciples' feet, and gave the "new commandment" (mandatum novum — from which the name "Maundy" derives) to love one another. Rites typically include: a Mass of the Lord's Supper or equivalent service, a Washing of Feet ceremony (mandatum), and the solemn transfer or "repose" of the Blessed Sacrament. In Catholic tradition, the altar is stripped bare at the end of the liturgy, signaling the beginning of the Triduum.

---

### Good Friday
*Also called: Viernes Santo, Vendredi Saint, Karfreitag, Great and Holy Friday (Orthodox), Crucifixion of Our Lord*

| Date | Type | Date Type | Denominations | Season | Region | Color | Themes |
|------|------|-----------|---------------|--------|--------|-------|--------|
| Friday of Holy Week | Feast, Fast Day | Moveable | Pan-Christian | Holy Week / Triduum | Global | Red (Catholic), Black (some traditions), no color (some Protestant) | Passion-Death, Holy Cross |

The most solemn day of the Christian year, commemorating the crucifixion and death of Jesus Christ at Calvary. In Catholic practice, there is no Mass on Good Friday — the service (the Liturgy of the Lord's Passion) consists of readings, the Veneration of the Cross, and Holy Communion from pre-consecrated hosts. Many Protestant traditions hold Tenebrae services (a service of darkening) or Stations of the Cross. Good Friday is one of two required fast-and-abstinence days in Catholicism. A public holiday in many countries worldwide. In Ethiopia, it is called Siqlet (Crucifixion) and is observed with particular intensity.

---

### Holy Saturday / Great and Holy Saturday
*Also called: Silent Saturday, Easter Eve, Sabbato Santo, Sábado Santo*

| Date | Type | Date Type | Denominations | Season | Region | Color | Themes |
|------|------|-----------|---------------|--------|--------|-------|--------|
| Saturday of Holy Week (before Easter Sunday) | Feast | Moveable | Pan-Christian | Holy Week / Triduum | Global | White (Easter Vigil), no liturgical services in daytime | Passion-Death, Resurrection, Eschatology |

The day between Christ's burial and resurrection — a day of silence, vigil, and anticipation. No Mass is celebrated during the daytime in the Catholic tradition; the only liturgy is the Easter Vigil, which begins after nightfall. The Easter Vigil is considered the "mother of all vigils" and includes the Service of Light (lighting the Paschal candle), the Liturgy of the Word with multiple Old Testament readings, and the Liturgy of Baptism. For Eastern Orthodox Christians, Holy Saturday includes the service called the "Lamentations" (Epitaphios), in which the burial shroud of Christ is venerated.

---

### Easter Sunday / Resurrection of Our Lord
*Also called: Pascha (Greek/Orthodox), Pesach/Pâques, Ostern (German), Påsk (Swedish), Паска (Ukrainian), Fasika (Ethiopian), Zatik (Armenian)*

| Date | Type | Date Type | Denominations | Season | Region | Color | Themes |
|------|------|-----------|---------------|--------|--------|-------|--------|
| First Sunday after the first full moon on or after March 21 (Western); calculated similarly against Julian calendar (Eastern — usually 1–5 weeks later) | Major Feast, Solemnity (Catholic), Principal Holy Day (Anglican) | Moveable | Pan-Christian | Easter | Global | White, Gold | Resurrection |

The central and most important feast of the entire Christian calendar, celebrating the resurrection of Jesus Christ from the dead on the third day after his crucifixion. All other seasons and feasts of the liturgical year radiate from Easter. In Eastern Orthodoxy, Pascha is described as the "Feast of Feasts" and "Resurrection of Resurrections." The night before (Easter Vigil / Paschal Vigil) is when catechumens are baptized and confirmed. Every Sunday of the year is considered a "little Easter" in Catholic and Orthodox theology. In Ethiopia and elsewhere, Easter follows a longer Lenten fast (55 days in Ethiopian practice).

---

### Easter Monday
*Also called: Lundi de Pâques, Ostermontag, Lunes de Pascua, Bright Monday (Orthodox)*

| Date | Type | Date Type | Denominations | Season | Region | Color | Themes |
|------|------|-----------|---------------|--------|--------|-------|--------|
| Monday after Easter Sunday | Feast | Moveable | Catholic, Eastern Orthodox, Anglican, Lutheran, most Western traditions | Easter | Europe, Latin America, many countries | White, Gold | Resurrection |

The day after Easter Sunday, widely observed as a public holiday and continuation of Easter celebrations across Europe, Latin America, Australia, and many other regions. In Poland, Śmigus-dyngus (Wet Monday) involves playful water-throwing between young people. In Eastern Orthodoxy, it is called Bright Monday, the first day of Bright Week. In many countries it is a formal bank holiday, though not a major liturgical day. In some traditions (particularly Methodist), an "Easter Monday lovefeast" was historically observed.

### Ascension Day / Ascension of Our Lord
*Also called: Ascensiontide, Feast of the Ascension, Jesu Himmelfahrt (German), Wniebowstąpienie (Polish)*

| Date | Type | Date Type | Denominations | Season | Region | Color | Themes |
|------|------|-----------|---------------|--------|--------|-------|--------|
| 40 days after Easter (traditionally Thursday; often transferred to the following Sunday in many Western countries) | Solemnity (Catholic), Major Feast / One of the Twelve Great Feasts (Orthodox), Principal Holy Day (Anglican) | Moveable | Pan-Christian (liturgical) | Easter / Ascension | Global | White | Ascension, Life of Christ |

The feast commemorating Jesus Christ's ascension into heaven 40 days after his resurrection, as described in Acts 1:9–11 and Luke 24:50–51. In Catholic tradition it remains on Thursday and is a Holy Day of Obligation in some countries; many dioceses have transferred it to the following Sunday. In Eastern Orthodox tradition it is one of the Twelve Great Feasts and is calculated as the 40th day after Pascha (always a Thursday). The feast marks Christ's glorification at the right hand of the Father and anticipates the sending of the Holy Spirit at Pentecost.

### Pentecost / Day of Pentecost
*Also called: Whit Sunday (British), Pfingsten (German), Pentecôte (French), Shavuot of Christians; Descent of the Holy Spirit (Orthodox)*

| Date | Type | Date Type | Denominations | Season | Region | Color | Themes |
|------|------|-----------|---------------|--------|--------|-------|--------|
| 50 days after Easter (7th Sunday after Easter) | Solemnity (Catholic), Major Feast (Orthodox), Principal Holy Day (Anglican) | Moveable | Pan-Christian | Pentecost | Global | Red | Holy Spirit, Mission, Church |

The feast of the descent of the Holy Spirit upon the apostles and disciples gathered in Jerusalem, as described in Acts 2:1–11. Often called the "birthday of the Church." In the Catholic and Anglican tradition, red vestments signify the tongues of fire of the Holy Spirit. In England, Whit Sunday was historically a major occasion for baptisms and Whit Monday was a public holiday. Pentecost Sunday is especially significant in Pentecostal and Charismatic Christianity, which places great emphasis on the gifts of the Spirit. In Eastern Orthodoxy, it is one of the Twelve Great Feasts and the liturgy includes a special service of kneeling prayers (Vespers of Pentecost).

---

### Whit Monday
*Also called: Whitmonday, Lundi de Pentecôte, Pfingstmontag, Whitsun Monday*

| Date | Type | Date Type | Denominations | Season | Region | Color | Themes |
|------|------|-----------|---------------|--------|--------|-------|--------|
| Monday after Pentecost Sunday | Feast | Moveable | Catholic, Anglican, Lutheran, many Protestant | Pentecost | Europe, Western Christianity | Red | Holy Spirit |

The day after Pentecost Sunday, observed as a public holiday in Germany, Austria, Switzerland, France, Hungary, the Netherlands, and many other European countries. Though it has largely secular significance today, it historically completed the Pentecost "octave" celebration and was a day for outdoor processions and Whitsun fairs in England. Some Lutheran and Catholic traditions observe the Monday as a continuation of the feast.

### All Saints' Day
*Also called: All Hallows' Day, Allerheiligen (German), La Toussaint (French), Todos los Santos (Spanish), Día de Todos los Santos*

| Date | Type | Date Type | Denominations | Season | Region | Color | Themes |
|------|------|-----------|---------------|--------|--------|-------|--------|
| November 1 | Solemnity (Catholic), Principal Holy Day (Anglican), Major Feast (Orthodox: 1st Sunday after Pentecost) | Fixed (Western); Semi-fixed (Eastern) | Catholic, Anglican, Lutheran, Methodist, Eastern Orthodox (different date) | Ordinary Time | Global | White | Martyrs-Saints |

A feast honoring all Christian saints, known and unknown, who have attained eternal life in heaven. In Catholicism it is a Holy Day of Obligation. The Anglican and Episcopal tradition celebrates it as one of the seven Principal Feasts. In Eastern Orthodoxy, All Saints Sunday falls on the first Sunday after Pentecost. In many cultures (particularly Mexican and Latin American), November 1 blends with the feast of All Souls and pre-Christian traditions into the Día de Muertos / Day of the Dead (see Regional section). In Poland, All Saints (Wszystkich Świętych) is a national public holiday when families visit graves with candles and flowers.

---

### All Souls' Day / Commemoration of the Faithful Departed
*Also called: Día de los Muertos (Mexico), Dzień Zaduszny (Poland), Giornata della Memoria, Seelensonntag (German)*

| Date | Type | Date Type | Denominations | Season | Region | Color | Themes |
|------|------|-----------|---------------|--------|--------|-------|--------|
| November 2 | Memorial (Catholic), Commemoration | Fixed | Catholic, Anglican (as a commemoration), Lutheran (ELCA), Orthodox (several Soul Saturdays) | Ordinary Time | Global | Black, Purple/Violet | Penitence, Eschatology, Martyrs-Saints |

The day after All Saints' Day, dedicated to prayer for all the faithful departed who are not yet in the full glory of heaven. In Catholic theology, this relates to the doctrine of Purgatory and the practice of praying for the dead. In Mexico and many Latin American cultures, November 1–2 is celebrated as Día de Muertos, a vibrant cultural and religious observance blending Aztec ancestor veneration with Catholic All Saints'/All Souls' traditions. Eastern Orthodoxy has multiple "Soul Saturdays" (Psych-Saturdays) throughout the year on which prayers are offered for the dead, rather than a single annual day.

## Section 2: Roman Catholic Holidays

*The Roman Catholic Church (Latin Rite) has the most elaborate liturgical calendar, organized into three ranks: Solemnities (highest), Feasts, and Memorials (obligatory and optional). A fourth category, Commemorations, exists for some observances.*

---

### Solemnity of Mary, Mother of God
*Also called: World Day of Peace; Feast of the Circumcision (former name)*

| Date | Type | Date Type | Denominations | Season | Region | Color | Themes |
|------|------|-----------|---------------|--------|--------|-------|--------|
| January 1 (Octave Day of Christmas) | Solemnity, Holy Day of Obligation | Fixed | Catholic; Anglican (as Holy Name); Lutheran (Circumcision and Name of Jesus) | Christmas | Global (Western Christianity) | White | Marian, Incarnation |

The first day of the calendar year is celebrated in the Catholic Church as a Marian solemnity, honoring Mary as the "Theotokos" (Mother of God). It is also the eighth day after Christmas (Octave Day), and recalls the circumcision of the infant Jesus and his naming (Luke 2:21). The Solemnity is a Holy Day of Obligation in the Catholic Church — though some bishops' conferences have abrogated this obligation when January 1 falls on a Saturday or Monday. The Pope traditionally issues a World Day of Peace message on this date. Previously in the pre-1970 calendar, this was the Feast of the Circumcision of Our Lord.

### Baptism of the Lord
*Also called: Feast of the Baptism of Christ*

| Date | Type | Date Type | Denominations | Season | Region | Color | Themes |

### Assumption of the Blessed Virgin Mary / Dormition of the Theotokos
*Also called: Dormition (Eastern), L'Assomption (French), Ferragosto (Italy), Mariä Himmelfahrt (German), Wniebowzięcie NMP (Polish), Asdvadzadzin (Armenian)*

The feast marking the end of the earthly life of the Virgin Mary and her bodily taking up (assumption/dormition) into heaven. ### Pascha / Orthodox Easter
*Also called: Resurrection of Christ, Feast of Feasts, Anastasis*

| Date | Type | Date Type | Denominations | Season | Region | Color | Themes |
|------|------|-----------|---------------|--------|--------|-------|--------|
| Calculated by Julian-based method; typically 1–5 weeks after Western Easter (occasionally same date) | The Feast of Feasts (above all Great Feasts) | Moveable | Eastern Orthodox | Easter | Eastern Christianity | White, Gold, Red | Resurrection |

In Eastern Orthodoxy, Pascha is not merely the most important feast — it is described as "the Feast of Feasts," standing apart from and above all the Twelve Great Feasts. The Paschal service begins at midnight on Holy Saturday, with the lighting of the Paschal candle and the proclamation "Christ is Risen!" (Christos Anesti! / Khristos Voskrese!), to which the congregation responds "Truly He is Risen!" The entirety of Bright Week (the week after Pascha) is treated as one continuous feast. Orthodox Pascha is calculated using Julian-calendar rules, placing it 1–5 weeks later than Western Easter in most years (though occasionally on the same date when lunar cycles align).

## Section 4: Oriental Orthodox

*The Oriental Orthodox churches — Coptic, Ethiopian, Armenian, and Syriac — separated from the Byzantine church in 451 AD following the Council of Chalcedon. They share deep liturgical heritage with Eastern Orthodoxy but have their own distinct calendars, languages, and traditions.*

---

### 4A: Coptic Orthodox (Egypt)

*The Coptic Orthodox Church uses the ancient Coptic (Alexandrian) calendar, closely aligned with the Julian calendar. The Coptic year begins on September 11 (Gregorian) with the Feast of Neyrouz. Coptic Christmas falls on January 7 (Gregorian), and Coptic Easter is computed by the same formula as Eastern Orthodox Pascha.*

---

### Feast of Neyrouz / Coptic New Year
*Also called: Feast of the Martyrs, First of Tut*

| Date | Type | Date Type | Denominations | Season | Region | Color | Themes |
|------|------|-----------|---------------|--------|--------|-------|--------|
| September 11 (Gregorian) / September 12 in leap years | National Feast / Ecclesiastical New Year | Fixed | Coptic Orthodox | Year-round | Egypt, Coptic diaspora | N/A | Church History, Martyrs-Saints |

The first day of the Coptic calendar (the month of Tut), and a commemoration of the many Christian martyrs who suffered under Emperor Diocletian (whose reign began in 284 AD, the Coptic Year 1). "Neyrouz" has no connection to the Persian spring festival Nowruz despite the similar name; it derives from the Coptic word for "the flowing of the Nile." Red-colored foods are traditionally eaten to symbolize the blood of the martyrs.

---

### Coptic Christmas / Nativity (Eid al-Milad)

| Date | Type | Date Type | Denominations | Season | Region | Color | Themes |
|------|------|-----------|---------------|--------|--------|-------|--------|
| January 7 (Gregorian) | Major Feast | Fixed | Coptic Orthodox | Christmas | Egypt, Coptic diaspora | White, Gold | Incarnation |

The Coptic celebration of the Nativity of Jesus Christ, following 43 days of fasting (the Advent/Nativity Fast, called Tsome Gahad). The Mass (Liturgy of St. Basil) begins late on Christmas Eve and continues through the night. The feast is a national public holiday in Egypt. Celebrated by approximately 10–15 million Coptic Christians in Egypt.

---

### Coptic Theophany / Epiphany (Eid al-Ghattas)

The Coptic celebration of Christ's Baptism in the Jordan River. A blessing of water is central to the feast. On the eve of the feast, a vigil is held and blessed water (Mayit al-Ghattas) is distributed to the faithful. The feast is also associated with the feast of St. John the Baptist.

### Flight into Egypt

The feast commemorating the Holy Family's flight to Egypt to escape Herod's massacre (Matthew 2:13–15), uniquely celebrated as a major feast in Egypt. The Coptic Church has an extensive tradition of the Holy Family's journey through Egypt — including specific cities, monasteries, and sites they are believed to have visited — spanning about 3.5 years. Pilgrimage routes following the Holy Family's path are an important part of Coptic spirituality.

### 4B: Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church

*The Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church uses the Ge'ez Ethiopian calendar, which is related to the Coptic calendar but has its own unique months and year numbering. The Ethiopian calendar is approximately 7–8 years behind the Gregorian calendar. Ethiopian Christmas falls on January 7 (Gregorian), and Easter (Fasika) is calculated by the same Coptic/Julian method.*

---

### Genna / Ledet (Ethiopian Christmas)
*Also called: ጌና (Genna), ልደት (Ledet)*

| Date | Type | Date Type | Denominations | Season | Region | Color | Themes |
|------|------|-----------|---------------|--------|--------|-------|--------|
| January 7 (Gregorian) / Tahsas 29 (Ethiopian) | Major Feast, National Public Holiday | Fixed | Ethiopian Orthodox | Christmas | Ethiopia | White | Incarnation |

The Ethiopian Orthodox celebration of the Nativity, observed on January 7, following 43 days of Advent fasting (Tsome Gahad). The name "Genna" is thought to derive from the word for "imminent" (relating to Christ's coming) and from a traditional hockey-like game (Yegena Chewata) played by men on this day. The liturgy begins very early in the morning (around 6 AM) with an elaborate procession. Men wear traditional white clothes (kemis and netela). Lalibela is a particularly famous site for Christmas celebrations, drawing pilgrims to its 12th-century rock-hewn churches.

---

### Timkat (Ethiopian Epiphany)
*Also called: Timkat Festival, ጥምቀት*

| Date | Type | Date Type | Denominations | Season | Region | Color | Themes |
|------|------|-----------|---------------|--------|--------|-------|--------|
| January 19 (Gregorian, or January 20 in leap years) | Major Feast, National Public Holiday | Fixed | Ethiopian Orthodox | Christmas | Ethiopia | White, Gold | Life of Christ, Baptism, Trinity |

One of the most spectacular religious celebrations in the world, Timkat commemorates Christ's Baptism in the Jordan River. The central ritual involves priests carrying the Tabot — a replica of the Ark of the Covenant — from the church to a nearby river or pool, covered with richly embroidered cloth. The procession begins on the eve of Timkat (Ketera), and thousands of pilgrims in white robes follow, chanting and dancing. The next morning, water is blessed and sprinkled on the faithful. Many worshippers immerse themselves or are splashed with holy water in a renewal of their baptism. UNESCO inscribed Timkat on the Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity list. The most famous Timkat celebration is held in Gondar, around the historic royal bath of Fasilides.

---

### Fasika (Ethiopian Easter)
*Also called: ፋሲካ, Easter of the Ethiopians*

| Date | Type | Date Type | Denominations | Season | Region | Color | Themes |
|------|------|-----------|---------------|--------|--------|-------|--------|
| First Sunday after the first full moon after March 21 (Coptic/Julian calculation) | Major Feast, National Public Holiday | Moveable | Ethiopian Orthodox | Easter | Ethiopia | White, Gold | Resurrection |

The most important religious celebration of the Ethiopian Orthodox calendar, preceded by the 55-day Great Lent (Hudade/Abye Tsome) — one of the strictest fasting periods of any Christian tradition. During the fast, Orthodox Ethiopians eat only once a day after 3 PM, with a completely vegan diet. The Easter Eve service (Ye'lel Qiddase) begins at 6 PM and continues through the night. After the Mass, the fast is broken with doro wat (spiced chicken stew) and tej (honey wine). Addis Ababa, Lalibela, Axum, and Gondar host the largest pilgrimages. Approximately 800,000 international visitors come to Ethiopia specifically for Fasika each year.

---

### Enkutatash (Ethiopian New Year / St. John the Baptist Day)
*Also called: ዓዲ, Kiddus Yohannes, September 11*

| Date | Type | Date Type | Denominations | Season | Region | Color | Themes |
|------|------|-----------|---------------|--------|--------|-------|--------|
| September 11 (Gregorian, or Sep 12 in leap years) / Meskerem 1 (Ethiopian) | National Feast / Ecclesiastical New Year | Fixed | Ethiopian Orthodox | Year-round | Ethiopia | N/A | Martyrs-Saints, Creation |

The Ethiopian New Year falls at the end of the long rainy season, when the highlands burst into color with golden Meskel daisies. "Enkutatash" means "gift of jewels," evoking the story of the Queen of Sheba returning from visiting King Solomon in Jerusalem and being welcomed back with jewels by her chiefs. The day also commemorates St. John the Baptist (Kiddus Yohannes). Children dress in new clothes and sing traditional songs while carrying bouquets of flowers from house to house. A service is held at church, after which priests read the new calendar (Bahre-Hassab). Celebrated equally by Christians and Muslims in Ethiopia.

---

### Meskel (Finding of the True Cross)
*Also called: Meskal, መስቀል, Demera Festival*

| Date | Type | Date Type | Denominations | Season | Region | Color | Themes |
|------|------|-----------|---------------|--------|--------|-------|--------|
| September 27 (Gregorian) / Meskerem 17 (Ethiopian) — public holiday also on September 28 | Major Feast, National Public Holiday | Fixed | Ethiopian Orthodox | Ordinary Time | Ethiopia | Red, Gold | Holy Cross |

The feast commemorating the discovery of the True Cross by Empress Helena, mother of Constantine, in 326 AD. According to Ethiopian tradition, part of the True Cross later found its way to Ethiopia and is enshrined at Gishen Mariam monastery. The central ritual is the Demera — the building and burning of a massive bonfire topped with crosses and the yellow Meskel daisies (Adey Ababa). Priests bless the bonfire, and the direction the smoke rises is interpreted as an omen for the coming year. After the fire dies, the faithful mark their foreheads with ash in the sign of the cross. The Meskel celebration at Meskel Square in Addis Ababa draws hundreds of thousands of people. UNESCO inscribed the Meskel celebration on the Intangible Cultural Heritage list in 2013.

---

### Great Lent / Hudade (Ethiopian)

| Date | Type | Date Type | Denominations | Season | Region | Color | Themes |
|------|------|-----------|---------------|--------|--------|-------|--------|
| 55 days before Fasika | Fasting Period | Moveable | Ethiopian Orthodox | Lent | Ethiopia | Purple | Penitence, Resurrection |

The Ethiopian Orthodox Great Lent, known as Hudade or Abye Tsome ("the Great Fast"), is 55 days long — longer than any other Christian tradition's Lenten fast. During this period, Ethiopian Orthodox Christians follow a strict vegan fast, eating only once a day after 3 PM (with exceptions on Saturdays and Sundays when a meal after the morning service is permitted). No meat, dairy, eggs, or other animal products are consumed. The fast is considered spiritually meritorious and is undertaken by the majority of Ethiopian Orthodox Christians.

### 4C: Armenian Apostolic Church

*The Armenian Apostolic Church is the world's oldest national church, established by the Apostles Thaddaeus and Bartholomew and formalized under Gregory the Illuminator (301 AD). It has five "Cardinal Feasts" (Taghavarner), unique liturgical traditions, and several feasts found in no other Christian tradition.*

---

### Christmas and Theophany (Armenian)
*Also called: Soorp Dzenoont (Armenian: Holy Birth), Theophany of Our Lord*

| Date | Type | Date Type | Denominations | Season | Region | Color | Themes |
|------|------|-----------|---------------|--------|--------|-------|--------|
| January 6 | Cardinal Feast (one of Five Major Feasts) | Fixed | Armenian Apostolic | Christmas | Armenia, Armenian diaspora | White, Gold | Incarnation, Trinity |

The Armenian church uniquely combines the Nativity and Epiphany on January 6, preserving an ancient pre-4th-century practice. The Western and most Eastern separation of Christmas (December 25) and Epiphany (January 6) was not adopted by the Armenian church. The January 6 date thus commemorates simultaneously the birth of Christ and his baptism (the two "epiphanies" of God). The traditional fast before Christmas (the Advent Fast) precedes this date. The Christmas Eve dinner (Khetum) features rice, fish, nevik (chard and chickpeas), and yogurt soup, eaten before the Divine Liturgy. Liturgy is in Classical Armenian (Grabar).

---

### Armenian Easter (Zatik)
*Also called: Surb Zatik, elebrate as Soorb Zadig, Zatig*

| Date | Type | Date Type | Denominations | Season | Region | Color | Themes |
|------|------|-----------|---------------|--------|--------|-------|--------|
| Calculated by Western (Gregorian) Easter formula (unlike Greek and Russian Orthodox) | Cardinal Feast | Moveable | Armenian Apostolic | Easter | Armenia, Armenian diaspora | White, Gold | Resurrection |

The most sacred feast of the Armenian calendar, celebrating the resurrection of Christ. The Armenian church follows the Western Easter calculation (not the Julian calendar method used by Greek and Russian Orthodox), so Armenian Easter usually coincides with Western Easter. However, unique Armenian practices surround it: the Great Lent (Medz Bahk) lasts 40 days plus Holy Week. The Arm of St. Gregory the Illuminator (a relic at the Mother See of Holy Etchmiadzin) is displayed during Holy Week.

---

### Transfiguration / Vardavar
*Also called: Festival of Roses, Water Festival, Vayfearavar*

| Date | Type | Date Type | Denominations | Season | Region | Color | Themes |
|------|------|-----------|---------------|--------|--------|-------|--------|
| 14 weeks (98 days) after Easter — always a Sunday | Cardinal Feast, National Public Holiday in Armenia | Moveable | Armenian Apostolic | Ordinary Time | Armenia, Armenian diaspora | White, Gold | Life of Christ |

The Armenian celebration of Christ's Transfiguration, placed 14 weeks after Easter rather than on the universal date of August 6. This feast absorbed an ancient pre-Christian Armenian holiday dedicated to Astghik, goddess of water and love, and thus retains the popular tradition of throwing water on one another (water fights in the streets). The theological connection is to the waters of baptism and the divine light of Tabor. In Armenia today, Vardavar is one of the most joyfully anticipated holidays, with city-wide water fights and outdoor celebrations. Priests bless rose-water during the liturgy. The day after Vardavar, Monday, is a "Merelots" (Memorial Day) when families visit graves.

---

### Assumption of the Theotokos / Asdvadzadzin
*Also called: Surp Asdvadzadzin ("Holy Mother of God"), Assumption of Mary (Armenian), Verapokhmoomn*

| Date | Type | Date Type | Denominations | Season | Region | Color | Themes |
|------|------|-----------|---------------|--------|--------|-------|--------|
| Third Sunday of August | Cardinal Feast, National Public Holiday in Armenia | Semi-fixed | Armenian Apostolic | Dormition Fast / Ordinary Time | Armenia, Armenian diaspora | White | Marian |

One of the Five Cardinal Feasts, celebrating the Assumption/Dormition of the Virgin Mary. The feast is preceded by a week-long fast (Navasard Fast). In Armenian popular tradition, it is strongly connected to the blessing of grapes and other first fruits of summer — the feast is celebrated outdoors with vineyard processions, the blessing of produce, and family feasts. The connection between the Virgin Mary and agricultural abundance is ancient in Armenian spirituality. Followed by nine days of memorial services (for the souls of the departed).

---

### Exaltation of the Holy Cross / Khatchverats
*Also called: Khachverats (Armenian), Feast of the Holy Cross*

| Date | Type | Date Type | Denominations | Season | Region | Color | Themes |
|------|------|-----------|---------------|--------|--------|-------|--------|
| Third Sunday of September, National Public Holiday (the following Monday is given off) | Cardinal Feast | Semi-fixed | Armenian Apostolic | Ordinary Time | Armenia | Red | Holy Cross |

One of the Five Cardinal Feasts, commemorating the recovery of the True Cross from the Persians by the Byzantine Emperor Heraclius in 628 AD and its triumphant return through Armenia to Jerusalem. A large cross decorated with basil is brought out in procession to a field, and four priests move to the four corners to bless the entire world. The feast follows a fast (the Fast of the Exaltation) the preceding week.

---

### Feast of the Holy Translators / Surp Tarkmanchats
*Also called: Saints Mesrop and Sahak Feast, Feast of the Armenian Alphabet*

| Date | Type | Date Type | Denominations | Season | Region | Color | Themes |
|------|------|-----------|---------------|--------|--------|-------|--------|
| Third Saturday of October | National Feast | Semi-fixed | Armenian Apostolic | Ordinary Time | Armenia, Armenian diaspora | White | Church History, Mission |

A feast unique to the Armenian church, honoring Mesrop Mashtots (360–440 AD), who created the Armenian alphabet, and Catholicos Sahak I (350–439 AD), who directed the first translation of the Bible into Armenian. Together they and their circle of scholars translated the Scriptures, Greek theological works, and many liturgical texts into Armenian, enabling an independent Armenian Christian civilization. The feast also honors the later theologians Yeghishe, Moses of Khoren, David the Philosopher, Gregory of Narek, and Nerses Shnorhali. There is no equivalent feast in any other Christian tradition — the act of translation itself is commemorated as a sacred achievement.

### Fast of St. Gregory the Illuminator

| Date | Type | Date Type | Denominations | Season | Region | Color | Themes |
|------|------|-----------|---------------|--------|--------|-------|--------|
| Monday – Saturday (week before the feast; varies by year) | Weeklong Fast | Semi-fixed | Armenian Apostolic | Ordinary Time | Armenia | N/A | Penitence, Church History, Saints |

A five-day fast honoring St. Gregory the Illuminator, the first Catholicos of Armenia, who is credited with converting the Armenian king Tiridates III to Christianity in 301 AD, making Armenia the first nation to adopt Christianity as a state religion. Gregory had been imprisoned in a pit (the Khor Virap, "Deep Well") for 15 years before being released to heal the king. The Feast of St. Gregory includes the solemn display of his relics (particularly the right arm, or "Holy Arm") at the Mother See of Etchmiadzin.

### 4D: Syriac Orthodox Church

*The Syriac Orthodox Church (headquartered in Damascus) traces its origins to the ancient see of Antioch, founded by St. Peter. Its liturgy is in Classical Syriac (Aramaic), the language closest to the Aramaic spoken by Jesus. The calendar is similar to the Eastern Orthodox calendar.*

---

### Nineveh Fast / Fast of the Ninevites
*Also called: Ba'uta d'Ninwe (Syriac), Rogation of the Ninevites*

| Date | Type | Date Type | Denominations | Season | Region | Color | Themes |

### St. Michael and All Angels

The feast of the archangels Michael, Gabriel, and Raphael (and all angels). "Michaelmas" is one of the traditional English quarter-days, marking the beginning of autumn terms at universities. In Catholic tradition, September 29 is "Ss. Michael, Gabriel and Raphael, Archangels."

## Section 6: Lutheran Holidays

*Lutheranism retains the Catholic liturgical seasons while simplifying the sanctoral calendar, placing greater emphasis on the proclaimed Word and the temporal cycle (seasons of the church year) over the commemoration of individual saints. Key distinctive features: Reformation Day (October 31) and a strong emphasis on Advent and Epiphany.*

---

### Reformation Day / Reformation Sunday
*Also called: Reformationstag (German), Day of the Reformation*

|------|------|-----------|---------------|--------|--------|-------|--------|

The commemoration of Martin Luther's posting of the Ninety-Five Theses on the door of the Castle Church in Wittenberg on October 31, 1517 — the event traditionally regarded as the beginning of the Protestant Reformation. The liturgical color is red (for the Holy Spirit and the Word of God). A public holiday in several German states. The 500th anniversary (2017) was celebrated worldwide. Lutherans give thanks for the recovery of the Gospel and the Scriptures in the vernacular. The hymn "A Mighty Fortress Is Our God" (Ein feste Burg ist unser Gott) by Luther is the quintessential Reformation Day anthem.

In the Lutheran calendar, the Transfiguration is celebrated on the Last Sunday after Epiphany as a bridge between the Epiphany season (Christ revealed) and Lent (Christ crucified). ## Section 7: Methodist / Wesleyan Holidays

*The United Methodist Church and other Wesleyan traditions follow the Revised Common Lectionary and celebrate the main seasons and feasts of the Western liturgical year. They add several distinctive observances rooted in the history of the Wesleyan revival.*

---

### Watch Night / Covenant Renewal Service
*Also called: New Year's Eve Service, Freedom Night*

| Date | Type | Date Type | Denominations | Season | Region | Color | Themes |
|------|------|-----------|---------------|--------|--------|-------|--------|
| December 31 (New Year's Eve) | Observance | Fixed | Methodist, some Baptist, Evangelical, Holiness traditions | Christmas | North America especially | White | Covenant, Eschatology |

A service of prayer, praise, and covenant renewal held on New Year's Eve, culminating at midnight. The tradition was established by John Wesley, who held the first Covenant Service for Methodists in 1755. Historically, enslaved African Americans in the USA used Watch Night on December 31, 1862 — the night before the Emancipation Proclamation took effect (January 1, 1863) — for prayer vigils, giving the service the additional name "Freedom Night." Many African American churches continue this tradition. The service includes renewal of Covenant promises: "I am no longer my own, but thine..." (Wesley's Covenant Prayer).

---

### Aldersgate Sunday
*Also called: Wesley Day, Aldersgate Day*

| Date | Type | Date Type | Denominations | Season | Region | Color | Themes |
|------|------|-----------|---------------|--------|--------|-------|--------|
| Sunday nearest May 24 | Observance | Semi-fixed | Methodist, Wesleyan | Easter / Ordinary Time | Global (Methodist) | White | Church History, Holy Spirit |

The commemoration of John Wesley's "Aldersgate experience" on May 24, 1738, when, during a meeting in Aldersgate Street, London, at which Luther's Preface to the Epistle to the Romans was being read, Wesley felt his "heart strangely warmed" and experienced an assurance of salvation. This moment is considered the pivotal event of the Evangelical Revival and the beginning of the Methodist movement. The Sunday nearest May 24 is observed in many Methodist congregations with reflections on grace, assurance, and the work of the Holy Spirit.

---

### World Communion Sunday
*Also called: First Sunday in October*

| Date | Type | Date Type | Denominations | Season | Region | Color | Themes |
|------|------|-----------|---------------|--------|--------|-------|--------|
| First Sunday of October | Observance | Semi-fixed | Methodist (originated), now also observed by many Presbyterian, Lutheran, Baptist, Anglican churches | Ordinary Time | Global | White | Covenant, Mission |

An interdenominational observance on the first Sunday of October, when Christians of different traditions celebrate the Lord's Supper in intentional solidarity with all believers worldwide. Originated in 1933 by Hugh Thomson Kerr of Shadyside Presbyterian Church, Pittsburgh, it was adopted by the Federal Council of Churches in 1940 and is now observed across many denominations. The day emphasizes the unity of all Christians at the common table.

## Section 8: Reformed, Presbyterian, and Baptist Traditions

*Reformed and Presbyterian traditions (Calvin, Zwingli, Knox) historically rejected most of the liturgical calendar as having no Biblical warrant, emphasizing the weekly Lord's Day as the primary Christian observance. However, in the 20th–21st centuries, most Reformed denominations have adopted or reintroduced the major Christian festivals. Baptist and many Evangelical traditions similarly vary widely.*

---

### The Lord's Day (Reformed)

| Date | Type | Date Type | Denominations | Season | Region | Color | Themes |
|------|------|-----------|---------------|--------|--------|-------|--------|
| Every Sunday | Observance / Sabbath | Fixed (weekly) | Presbyterian, Reformed, Baptist, Evangelical | Year-round | Global | N/A | Resurrection, Covenant |

The weekly celebration of Sunday as the Christian Sabbath and the "little Easter" — the day of Christ's resurrection and of his appearance to the disciples. Reformed and Presbyterian theology has traditionally placed the primary emphasis here, not on the annual calendar, but on the weekly rhythm of Word preaching, prayer, and communion. The Westminster Confession of Faith (1647) teaches that Sunday is "the Christian Sabbath" and that one day in seven is holy.

---

### Resurrection Sunday (Evangelical/Baptist)

| Date | Type | Date Type | Denominations | Season | Region | Color | Themes |
|------|------|-----------|---------------|--------|--------|-------|--------|
| Same as Western Easter | Major Observance | Moveable | Baptist, Evangelical, Pentecostal, Fundamentalist | Easter | Global | N/A | Resurrection |

Many Baptist and Evangelical churches prefer the term "Resurrection Sunday" over "Easter" (which some associate with pre-Christian spring festivals and the name of the Anglo-Saxon goddess Ēostre). The service emphasizes the bodily resurrection of Christ from the dead, typically with special music, dramatic presentations, and large attendance. Unlike Catholic and liturgical traditions, most Baptist and Evangelical churches observe minimal additional Holy Week services beyond a Good Friday service and the Sunday celebration.

---

### Reformation Sunday (Reformed/Presbyterian)

| Date | Type | Date Type | Denominations | Season | Region | Color | Themes |
|------|------|-----------|---------------|--------|--------|-------|--------|

Observed by many Reformed and Presbyterian churches, this Sunday commemorates the Protestant Reformation and the principles of sola scriptura (Scripture alone), sola fide (faith alone), sola gratia (grace alone), and solus Christus (Christ alone). Special readings from the Reformers, sermons on the history of the Reformation, and singing of Reformation-era hymns (including Calvin's versification of the Psalms) are common.

---

### National Day of Prayer (USA)

| Date | Type | Date Type | Denominations | Season | Region | Color | Themes |
|------|------|-----------|---------------|--------|--------|-------|--------|

National Day of Prayer events are coordinated by the National Day of Prayer Task Force and held at courthouses, capitol buildings, and churches. Distinct from a liturgical feast, it is a civic-religious observance.

## Section 9: Pentecostal and Charismatic Christianity

*Pentecostal and Charismatic churches (Assembly of God, Church of God, Foursquare, and others) generally observe few formal liturgical holy days, but attach great importance to Pentecost Sunday as the founding event of their movement. They celebrate Christmas and Easter / Resurrection Sunday, and often observe Good Friday.*

---

### Pentecost Sunday (Pentecostal Emphasis)

| Date | Type | Date Type | Denominations | Season | Region | Color | Themes |
|------|------|-----------|---------------|--------|--------|-------|--------|
| 50 days after Easter | Principal Observance | Moveable | Pentecostal, Charismatic, all Christians | Pentecost | Global | Red | Holy Spirit, Mission |

Pentecost Sunday carries particular theological weight in Pentecostal traditions, as it commemorates the outpouring of the Holy Spirit that gave rise to speaking in tongues (glossolalia), prophecy, healing, and the charismata (spiritual gifts) — the experiences that define Pentecostal worship. Pentecostal churches often observe Pentecost with extended worship services, testimonies, prayer for the gifts of the Spirit, and evangelistic outreach. The 1906 Azusa Street Revival (Los Angeles) — which launched modern Pentecostalism — is sometimes recalled on this Sunday.

April 14, 1906 is the date of the initial outpouring of the Holy Spirit at 312 Azusa Street, Los Angeles, under the leadership of William J. While not a formal liturgical feast, this date is recognized in many Pentecostal and Charismatic churches, particularly around anniversaries (the centennial in 2006 saw major global celebrations).

## Section 10: Seventh-day Adventist Observances

*The Seventh-day Adventist Church observes the Sabbath on Saturday (from Friday sunset to Saturday sunset) as its central weekly observance, distinguishing it from most other Christian denominations. Adventists celebrate Christmas and Easter as cultural/theological observances, but place less emphasis on the traditional liturgical calendar. They have several unique annual emphases.*

---

### The Sabbath (Saturday)

| Date | Type | Date Type | Denominations | Season | Region | Color | Themes |
|------|------|-----------|---------------|--------|--------|-------|--------|
| Every Saturday (from Friday sunset to Saturday sunset) | Primary Weekly Observance | Fixed (weekly) | Seventh-day Adventist, some Messianic Jewish believers | Year-round | Global | N/A | Creation, Covenant |

The Seventh-day Sabbath is the central distinctive observance of the Adventist faith, based on the Fourth Commandment (Exodus 20:8–11) and the Genesis account of God resting on the seventh day of creation. The Adventist Sabbath includes corporate worship on Saturday morning, Bible study (Sabbath School), and a day set apart from ordinary work and commerce. It begins at sunset Friday and ends at sunset Saturday. Adventists view Sabbath observance as a sign of loyalty to God and an anticipation of the eternal "Sabbath rest" described in Hebrews 4.

## Section 11: Regional and National Christian Traditions

*Many Christian observances are specific to particular countries or cultures, blending universal Christian feasts with local heritage.*

---

### Simbang Gabi (Philippines)
*Also called: Misa de Aguinaldo, Night Mass, Misa de Gallo (final night)*

| Date | Type | Date Type | Denominations | Season | Region | Color | Themes |
|------|------|-----------|---------------|--------|--------|-------|--------|
| December 16–24 (nine days of pre-dawn Masses) | Regional Tradition / Novena of Masses | Fixed | Catholic (primary), some Protestant, Methodist churches in the Philippines | Advent | Philippines, Filipino diaspora | White, Violet | Incarnation, Marian |

A devotional series of nine pre-dawn Masses (novena) held from December 16 to 24, unique to Filipino Catholic practice. Originally instituted during the Spanish colonial era so that farmers could attend Mass before working in the fields. The Masses begin as early as 4 AM. Outside the churches, vendors sell traditional delicacies: bibingka (coconut rice cakes) and puto bumbong (steamed purple rice). Completing all nine Masses is believed to bring special graces or the granting of a wish. The final Mass (Misa de Gallo, "Rooster's Mass") on Christmas Eve precedes the nochebuena feast. Simbang Gabi is considered the world's longest Christmas celebration period — in the Philippines, the "ber months" (September–December) are informally the Christmas season.

---

### Las Posadas (Mexico / Latin America)
*Also called: Posadas, Posadas Navideñas*

| Date | Type | Date Type | Denominations | Season | Region | Color | Themes |
|------|------|-----------|---------------|--------|--------|-------|--------|
| December 16–24 (nine nights) | Regional Tradition / Novena | Fixed | Catholic | Advent | Mexico, Central America, USA (Mexican-American communities) | White, Gold | Incarnation |

A nine-night re-enactment of Mary and Joseph's search for lodging (posada) in Bethlehem before the birth of Jesus. Each night, participants split into two groups: pilgrims (singing the Litany of the Posadas asking for shelter) and innkeepers (refusing entry in song). The procession ends at a home or church where a manger scene (nacimiento) is set up. Piñatas (traditionally seven-pointed stars representing the seven deadly sins), traditional food (ponche, tamales), and singing mark the festivities. Las Posadas has pre-Columbian roots combined with Augustinian missionary adaptations from the 16th century.

---

### Día de Reyes / Three Kings Day (Spain, Latin America)
*Also called: Día de los Reyes Magos, Epifanía, La Cabalgata de Reyes*

| Date | Type | Date Type | Denominations | Season | Region | Color | Themes |
|------|------|-----------|---------------|--------|--------|-------|--------|
| January 6 | National Feast / Cultural Holiday | Fixed | Catholic | Christmas | Spain, Latin America, Puerto Rico, Caribbean | White, Gold | Incarnation, Life of Christ |

In Spain and most Latin American countries, January 6 (Epiphany) is the traditional gift-giving day, not December 25. The "Wise Men" (Reyes Magos: Melchior, Gaspar, and Balthasar) are believed to deliver gifts to children on the night of January 5–6. The night before, children leave shoes and sometimes straw (for the camels) by their beds. La Cabalgata de Reyes (Three Kings' Parade) on the evening of January 5 is a major public event in Spanish cities, with floats, costumes, and candy thrown to the crowd. The Rosca de Reyes (a circular sweet bread with hidden figures) is eaten at family gatherings on January 6.

### Wigilia (Poland)
*Also called: Christmas Eve Supper, Wieczerza Wigilijna*

| Date | Type | Date Type | Denominations | Season | Region | Color | Themes |
|------|------|-----------|---------------|--------|--------|-------|--------|
| December 24 (Christmas Eve) | Regional Tradition | Fixed | Catholic | Christmas | Poland, Polish diaspora | White | Incarnation |

The most important family gathering in the Polish Catholic year, observed on Christmas Eve (Wigilia means "Vigil"). The meal begins when the first star appears in the evening sky (recalling the Star of Bethlehem). Families share the opłatek, a thin white wafer embossed with a nativity scene, breaking and sharing it with each person while exchanging wishes. A traditional 12-course meatless meal follows (one course for each apostle), including borscht, carp, pierogi, herring, mushroom soup, and poppy-seed cake. An empty place is set at the table for an unexpected traveler or a deceased relative.

*Also called: Dyngus Day, Lany Poniedziałek (Splashing Monday)*

A uniquely Polish Easter Monday tradition in which people (especially boys) drench one another with water — originally by young men throwing buckets of water on girls they admired. The tradition predates Christianity and was absorbed into the Easter celebration as a symbol of baptism and renewal. In modern Poland and Polish-American communities, Dyngus Day is celebrated as a festive cultural holiday.

### Semana Santa (Spain / Latin America)
*Also called: Holy Week Processions, Procesiones de Semana Santa*

| Date | Type | Date Type | Denominations | Season | Region | Color | Themes |
|------|------|-----------|---------------|--------|--------|-------|--------|
| Palm Sunday through Easter Sunday | Regional Tradition | Moveable | Catholic | Holy Week | Spain, Latin America, Philippines | Various | Passion-Death, Resurrection |

Spain's Holy Week observances are among the most dramatic in the world, marked by elaborate public processions (procesiones) organized by religious brotherhoods (cofradías / hermandades). Large floats (pasos) bearing ornate statues of Christ and the Virgin Mary are carried through the streets by costaleros (bearers hidden underneath). Brotherhoods march in procession wearing distinctive pointed hoods (capirotes) and robes. The most famous celebrations are in Seville, Málaga, Zamora, Valladolid, and Granada. The "Madrugá" (early morning processions) on Holy Thursday night through Good Friday morning, ending at dawn, are the most emotionally intense. Similar traditions exist throughout Latin America.

### Our Lady of Fátima

The feast of the famous icon of the Black Madonna enshrined at Jasna Góra Monastery in Częstochowa, Poland. |------|------|-----------|---------------|--------|--------|-------|--------|
| January 9 | National Feast (Philippines) | Fixed | Catholic | Christmas / Epiphany | Philippines | Red, Maroon | Passion-Death, Life of Christ |

The feast of the Black Nazarene, a dark-colored statue of Jesus Christ carrying the cross, enshrined in the Minor Basilica of Quiapo, Manila. The statue arrived from Mexico in 1767 and is believed to be miraculous. On January 9, an estimated nine million barefoot devotees participate in the Traslación — a procession re-enacting the transfer of the statue from Quirino Grandstand to Quiapo Church. Devotees wear maroon shirts (the color of the Nazarene's robe) and barefoot as a sign of devotion. The procession can take over 20 hours due to the massive crowds. It is considered one of the largest religious processions in the world.

### Sinulog Festival (Philippines)
*Also called: Feast of the Santo Niño de Cebú, Feast of the Holy Child*

| Date | Type | Date Type | Denominations | Season | Region | Color | Themes |
|------|------|-----------|---------------|--------|--------|-------|--------|
| Third Sunday of January | National Feast (Philippines) | Semi-fixed | Catholic | Christmas / Epiphany | Philippines (Cebu) | N/A | Incarnation, Life of Christ |

The feast of the Santo Niño (Holy Child Jesus) of Cebu — the oldest statue of Christianity in the Philippines, given by Ferdinand Magellan to the Queen of Cebu in 1521 and recovered when Legaspi arrived in 1565. The Sinulog festival features enormous street dancing competitions in which dancers replicate the sinulog rhythm (a forward-and-backward motion recalling river currents) wearing elaborate costumes. The festival draws over a million participants and is considered one of the most colorful in Southeast Asia. A Solemn Pontifical Mass is celebrated at the Basilica Minore del Santo Niño.

### Mothering Sunday (United Kingdom)
*Also called: Mid-Lent Sunday, Laetare Sunday, Fourth Sunday of Lent*

| Date | Type | Date Type | Denominations | Season | Region | Color | Themes |
|------|------|-----------|---------------|--------|--------|-------|--------|
| Fourth Sunday of Lent (mid-Lent) | Feast / Custom | Moveable | Anglican, Catholic, Methodist (UK) | Lent | United Kingdom, Ireland | Rose/Pink | Church History, Marian |

A tradition with pre-Reformation origins, when servants were given the day off to visit their "mother church" (the cathedral or church of baptism). Families reunited and the day became an occasion for celebration of mothers. The liturgical color rose/pink is used on this Sunday (called "Laetare Sunday," from the opening word of the introit "Laetare Jerusalem" — "Rejoice, Jerusalem"). Traditional simnel cake (a rich fruit cake decorated with marzipan) is eaten. In the Anglican tradition, the Epistle of the day is Galatians 4:21–31 (on the "mother" Jerusalem). Now widely observed as the UK equivalent of Mother's Day.

### St. Nicholas Day
*Also called: Sinterklaas (Netherlands/Belgium), Nikolaustag (Germany), Feast of St. Nicholas*

| Date | Type | Date Type | Denominations | Season | Region | Color | Themes |
|------|------|-----------|---------------|--------|--------|-------|--------|
| December 6 | Optional Memorial (Catholic), Festival (Lutheran) | Fixed | Catholic, Lutheran, Eastern Orthodox (December 19 Julian), Dutch Reformed (cultural) | Advent | Netherlands, Belgium, Germany, Austria, Eastern Europe | White | Martyrs-Saints |

The feast of St. Nicholas, Bishop of Myra (c. 270–343 AD), known for his generosity to the poor (including secretly providing dowries for three girls, throwing bags of gold through their window). St. Nicholas is the historical prototype of the modern "Santa Claus" figure (Sinterklaas → Santa Claus). In the Netherlands and Belgium, Sinterklaas arrives by boat from Spain on November 19, parades through cities on a white horse, and brings gifts to children on the evening of December 5 (Sinterklaas Eve). In Germany and Austria, children put shoes out on December 5 to be filled with sweets by "Father Christmas" (Nikolaus). The Eastern Orthodox Church observes the feast on December 6 (Old Calendar: December 19).

On the eve of St. Nicholas Day (December 6), the companion figure of Krampus (a horned creature representing punishment for naughty children) traditionally accompanies St. Nicholas. ### Feast of St. Francis of Assisi
*Also called: Blessing of the Animals*

| Date | Type | Date Type | Denominations | Season | Region | Color | Themes |

The feast of St. Francis of Assisi (1181–1226), founder of the Franciscan Order, patron of ecology and animals, known for his "Canticle of the Sun" and his embodiment of evangelical poverty. While Corpus Christi is a universal Catholic feast, its outdoor processions take on special character in Bavaria, Austria, and Poland, where entire towns decorate their streets with flower carpets and elaborate altars. ### Day of the Dead / Día de Muertos (Mexico)
*Also called: Día de los Muertos, Todos Santos, Ofrenda tradition*

| Date | Type | Date Type | Denominations | Season | Region | Color | Themes |
|------|------|-----------|---------------|--------|--------|-------|--------|
| November 1–2 (coinciding with All Saints' and All Souls' Days) | Regional Tradition / Cultural-Religious Observance | Fixed | Catholic (core), combined with pre-Columbian Aztec traditions | Ordinary Time | Mexico, Central America, Chicano communities in USA | Multicolor (marigolds, purple, orange) | Penitence, Eschatology, Martyrs-Saints |

A Mexican and Central American observance blending Catholic All Saints' and All Souls' commemorations with pre-Columbian Aztec ancestor veneration (particularly the Aztec Feast of the Dead, originally in summer). Families build elaborate altars (ofrendas) decorated with marigold flowers (cempasúchil), photographs of deceased loved ones, food, candles, and the deceased's favorite items. The belief is that on these nights, the spirits of the dead return to visit the living. UNESCO inscribed it as Intangible Cultural Heritage in 2008. The tradition has spread beyond Mexico through cultural exchange and is now observed in many US communities.

### Feast of St. Thomas, Apostle (India)
*Also called: Feast of Doubting Thomas, St. Thomas the Apostle, Mar Thoma*

| Date | Type | Date Type | Denominations | Season | Region | Color | Themes |
|------|------|-----------|---------------|--------|--------|-------|--------|
| July 3 (Catholic, Anglican, Lutheran); October 6 (Eastern Orthodox) | Feast (Catholic/Anglican), Festival (Lutheran) | Fixed | Catholic, Anglican, Lutheran, Eastern Orthodox, Mar Thoma Church (India) | Ordinary Time | India (especially Kerala) | Red | Apostles, Church History |

The feast of St. Thomas the Apostle, who according to ancient tradition brought Christianity to India in 52 AD and was martyred in Chennai (Madras) in 72 AD. For the Thomas Christians of Kerala (India), the oldest Christian community in Asia, this feast is of extraordinary importance — they trace their faith directly to Thomas. The Basilica of the National Shrine of St. Thomas at Mylapore (Chennai) is a major pilgrimage site. Thomas' role as the one who doubted and then proclaimed "My Lord and my God" upon touching Christ's wounds has given him particular devotion as patron of those with intellectual struggles in faith.

### Feast of St. Francis Xavier (Asia, Goa)

| Date | Type | Date Type | Denominations | Season | Region | Color | Themes |
|------|------|-----------|---------------|--------|--------|-------|--------|
| December 3 | Memorial (Catholic), Feast in Goa | Fixed | Catholic | Advent | Goa (India), Japan, Macau, Philippines, Asia broadly | White, Red | Martyrs-Saints, Mission |

The feast of St. Francis Xavier (1506–1552), Jesuit missionary and "Apostle of the Indies," co-founder of the Jesuits with Ignatius Loyola. He is the patron of Christian missions, Goa, Navarre (Spain), Japan, and co-patron of the Philippines. His incorrupt body is enshrined at the Basilica of Bom Jesus in Old Goa, India, and is exposed to the public every ten years (the "Exposition"). The feast is a public holiday in Goa. Francis Xavier baptized thousands throughout India, Japan, and Southeast Asia during his 10 years of mission (1542–1552).

### Orthodox Easter Celebrations (Greece — Local Color)

| Date | Type | Date Type | Denominations | Season | Region | Color | Themes |
|------|------|-----------|---------------|--------|--------|-------|--------|
| Orthodox Pascha (moveable) | Major Feast with Regional Traditions | Moveable | Eastern Orthodox | Easter | Greece | White, Gold | Resurrection |

The Greek Orthodox Easter combines liturgical solemnity with vivid popular traditions. The midnight service of Pascha ("Anastasis") sees worshippers holding candles lit from the "Holy Fire" (believed to descend miraculously at the Church of the Holy Sepulchre in Jerusalem each Holy Saturday). The proclamation "Christos Anesti!" (Christ is Risen!) is met with "Alithos Anesti!" (Truly He is Risen!). On Easter Sunday, families roast whole lambs (spit-roasted arnaki), and Easter eggs (dyed red to symbolize the blood of Christ) are cracked in the game tsougrisma (knocking eggs together, the winner's egg remaining intact). The Easter table features tsoureki (braided sweet bread), kokoretsi, and magiritsa (lamb offal soup eaten after the midnight service).

### Holy Fire Ceremony (Jerusalem)

| Date | Type | Date Type | Denominations | Season | Region | Color | Themes |
|------|------|-----------|---------------|--------|--------|-------|--------|
| Holy Saturday (Orthodox calendar) | Ceremony / Miraculous Event | Moveable | Eastern Orthodox (primarily), some Oriental Orthodox | Holy Week | Jerusalem, broadcast globally | N/A | Resurrection |

The Holy Fire (Holy Light) ceremony at the Church of the Holy Sepulchre in Jerusalem is one of the most ancient rituals in Eastern Christianity, attested since the 4th century. The Greek Orthodox Patriarch enters the Tomb of Christ (the Edicule) and emerges carrying two lighted candles, believed by the faithful to have been miraculously lit by divine fire. The fire is distributed throughout the congregation and flown to Orthodox capitals worldwide (Athens, Moscow, etc.) for distribution to the faithful. The ceremony is broadcast live on television across Orthodox countries.

| Date | Type | Date Type | Denominations | Season | Region | Color | Themes |
|------|------|-----------|---------------|--------|--------|-------|--------|
| January 27 | National Feast (Serbia) | Fixed | Serbian Orthodox | Christmas | Serbia, Serbian diaspora | White | Martyrs-Saints, Church History |

The feast of St. Sava (Nemanjić, 1174–1236), founder of the Serbian Orthodox Church and the first Archbishop of Serbia, patron of the Serbs, of education, and of children. Sveti Sava Day is one of the most important days in the Serbian Orthodox calendar and is associated particularly with schools (children bring offerings to their teachers). The Shrine of St. Sava on Vračar Hill in Belgrade (the largest Orthodox church in the world) is dedicated to him.

### Theophany Blessing of Waters (Russia / Eastern Europe)
*See also: Theophany / Orthodox Epiphany (Section 3)*

| Date | Type | Date Type | Denominations | Season | Region | Color | Themes |
|------|------|-----------|---------------|--------|--------|-------|--------|
| January 19 (Gregorian) — two weeks after Julian Christmas | Regional Tradition | Fixed | Russian Orthodox, Ukrainian Orthodox, Bulgarian Orthodox | Christmas | Russia, Ukraine, Belarus, Serbia, Bulgaria | White | Life of Christ, Trinity |

In Russia and across Slavic Orthodox countries, the Theophany Blessing of Waters on January 19 is marked by a beloved popular tradition: the cutting of ice-hole crosses (iordani) in frozen rivers and lakes, through which the faithful plunge in the icy water as a sign of devotion and renewed baptismal faith. Millions participate across Russia, Ukraine, and Belarus, particularly at significant rivers. Holy water (Agiasmos/Svyataya Voda) blessed on this day is kept by Orthodox families throughout the year for blessings, illnesses, and sacred purposes.

## Appendix A: Date Calculation Reference

### Western Easter (Catholic, Protestant, Anglican, Lutheran, Methodist)
Easter = First Sunday after the first Ecclesiastical Full Moon (the 14th day of the moon) on or after March 21. Cannot fall before March 22 or after April 25.

### Eastern / Orthodox Pascha
Same algorithm but applied to the Julian calendar, with the additional rule that Pascha must always fall after the Jewish Passover (Pesach). The Julian calendar currently runs 13 days behind the Gregorian calendar, pushing Orthodox Pascha typically 1–5 weeks later. Occasionally Pascha coincides with Western Easter (same date) when the lunar cycles align.

### Armenian Easter
The Armenian Apostolic Church uses the same calculation as Western (Gregorian) Easter, not the Julian-based Orthodox formula.

### Coptic / Ethiopian Easter
Calculated using the Alexandrian method, which is effectively the same as the Julian Orthodox method, so it coincides with Eastern Orthodox Pascha in most years.

## Appendix C: Glossary of Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Solemnity** | Highest-ranked celebration in the Catholic calendar; replaces any Sunday except the four Sundays of Advent and the Sundays of Lent and Easter |
| **Feast** | Second rank in the Catholic calendar; observed with special readings and prayers but yields to Sundays in Ordinary Time |
| **Memorial** | Third rank; obligatory memorials replace the weekday, optional memorials may be observed |
| **Holy Day of Obligation** | A day on which Catholics are bound to attend Mass in addition to Sundays |
| **Great Feast** | One of the Twelve Great Feasts of the Orthodox Church (plus Pascha, the Feast of Feasts) |
| **Pascha** | The Greek/Orthodox name for Easter, emphasizing its connection to the Jewish Passover (Pesach) |
| **Theotokos** | Greek: "God-bearer" / "Mother of God" — the Orthodox title for the Virgin Mary |
| **Triduum** | The "Three Days" from the evening of Maundy Thursday through Easter Sunday |
| **Octave** | An eight-day celebration of a major feast (e.g., the Octave of Easter; formerly the Octave of Christmas through January 1) |
| **Novena** | Nine days of prayer before a feast; e.g., Simbang Gabi (nine Masses before Christmas) |
| **Omoforion** | The liturgical vestment of an Orthodox bishop, analogous to the Western pallium |
| **Tabot** | A replica of the Ark of the Covenant kept in every Ethiopian Orthodox church |
| **Grabar** | Classical Armenian, the liturgical language of the Armenian Apostolic Church |
| **Ge'ez** | The Classical Ethiopic liturgical language of the Ethiopian Orthodox Church |
| **Revised Common Lectionary** | A three-year cycle of Scripture readings used by Catholic, Anglican, Lutheran, Methodist, Presbyterian, and other churches |
| **Temporal Cycle** | The part of the liturgical calendar focused on seasons and events in the life of Christ |
| **Sanctoral Cycle** | The part of the liturgical calendar focused on feasts of Mary and the saints |
| **Computus** | The calculation used to determine the date of Easter |
| **Fixed Feast** | A feast that occurs on the same calendar date every year |
| **Moveable Feast** | A feast whose date changes each year (typically relative to Easter) |
| **Fast Day** | A day of abstinence from food (or certain foods) as an act of prayer and penance |
